Step 3 · Confirm understanding and capture the flag

Answer the questions to validate your investigation. Expand the disclosures if you need to double-check the logic.

Question 1 · Platform escalation

Which behaviour most clearly indicates an attempted account takeover?

  • A. Asking for webinar slides.
  • B. Requesting to move to WhatsApp for payment processing.
  • C. Thanking you for your time.
  • D. Sharing a conference invite.
Reveal answer
Correct: B — attackers often pivot to unmonitored channels to share malicious links.

Question 2 · Link safety

What should you do before opening the shortened payment link?

  • A. Paste directly into browser to test it.
  • B. Forward it to friends to see their reaction.
  • C. Expand it in a safe preview tool to inspect the destination.
  • D. Ignore it and hope for the best.
Reveal answer
Correct: C — always expand shortened links in a safe environment before visiting.

Question 3 · Country code mismatch

Why is the +44 number concerning for this scenario?

  • A. Because +44 numbers are premium-rate.
  • B. Your company operates only in North America.
  • C. The number matches the executive sponsor’s direct line.
  • D. International numbers always mean fraud.
Reveal answer
Correct: B — threat actors often register foreign numbers to bypass local trust checks.

Question 4 · Containment

Which action best reduces ongoing exposure?

  • A. Reply to the attacker asking for more time.
  • B. Block the phone number and report the profile through official channels.
  • C. Post the link publicly to warn others.
  • D. Save the number as a trusted contact.
Reveal answer
Correct: B — blocking and reporting removes attacker access and alerts platform trust teams.

Question 5 · Communication

Who should receive your incident summary first?

  • A. Public relations to draft a press release.
  • B. Finance leadership through the official incident channel.
  • C. Marketing interns for awareness.
  • D. External social media followers.
Reveal answer
Correct: B — notify accountable leaders via official channels before broader awareness.

TryHackMe-style Module Breakdown

Use these modules to reinforce the lab topics. Each includes visuals, guided reading, and quick wins.

Module 1

Assessing Social Profiles

This module focuses on evaluating social media and professional networking profiles before accepting connection requests or engaging in conversation. You will learn how attackers build synthetic personas, reuse stolen images, and spoof employer details to gain trust and deliver phishing links or malicious attachments.

  • Apply reverse-image search best practices to detect reused profile pictures, stock images, or identities copied from real people.
  • Use a structured authenticity checklist—profile history, mutual connections, posting patterns, and domain consistency—to validate whether a persona is genuine.
  • Follow safe escalation routes when in doubt, including verifying identities via corporate channels or informing security teams about suspicious outreach.
Module 2

Secure Messaging Hygiene

This module covers best practices for staying safe on corporate messaging apps such as WhatsApp, Signal, Slack, and Microsoft Teams. You will learn how attackers insert malicious links, impersonate colleagues, and exploit fast-paced chat environments to socially engineer employees. The focus is on applying structured verification and documentation workflows to keep conversations secure.

  • Use out-of-band verification flows to confirm unexpected requests—such as payment approvals, document sign-offs, or link shares—via known corporate numbers or internal communication tools.
  • Follow predefined reporting templates to escalate suspicious messages to platform abuse teams or internal security operations with the right artefacts attached.
  • Apply safe blocking and logging procedures to record sender details, capture screenshots, and file chat transcripts for SOC review and audit compliance.
Module 3

Threat Intel Correlation

This module teaches you how to connect the dots across multiple incidents by correlating attacker-controlled assets such as phone numbers, domains, usernames, and social media handles. By tracing these shared indicators, you can identify coordinated phishing campaigns and recognize attacker infrastructure that repeatedly targets your organization.

  • Pivot between chat logs, email headers, profile links, and metadata to uncover reused artefacts and establish relationships between seemingly separate events.
  • Build repeatable enrichment workflows using threat intelligence sources—passive DNS, OSINT platforms, breach data, and reputation feeds—to validate suspicious identities.
  • Share intelligence responsibly with SOC teams, trusted peers, and information-sharing groups while maintaining compliance with data sensitivity and privacy guidelines.
